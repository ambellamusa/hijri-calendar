This website has been created with the aim of reviving the Sunnah of using the Hijri calendar, which is deeply rooted in the Islamic tradition. Many Muslims today have become accustomed to using the Gregorian calendar, often forgetting the importance of the Islamic calendar. Here, we will highlight key Islamic months, dates, and events from the Prophetic Sunnah, allowing us to familiarize ourselves with significant moments from our history and strengthen our connection to Islamic times and dates.

Islamic Calendar

Al-Muharram (المُحَرَّم) 🔴▼

Al-Muharram is so called because it is a sacred (muharram meaning "forbidden") month and is one of the four sacred months in Islam.

Important Dates▼

Ashura (10th of Muharram)

Virtues of this Month▼

Fasting on Ashura is highly virtuous.

Recommended Actions▼

Fasting on the 9th and 10th of Muharram.

Safar (صَفَر) ▼

The word Safar means "void" or "empty." Pre-Islamic Arabs named it thus because their homes would often be empty during this time due to fighting or travelling.

Rabī' Al-Awwal (رَبِيعُ الأَوَّل) ▼

Rabi' al-Awwal translates to "the first spring,". It is so called because they did not travel during that month (the word irtibaa‘, derived from the same root, means “not travelling”). It is also the month in which the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ was born. However, it is important to note that celebrating his birth was not done by the companions, as doing so is regarded as an innovation (bid’ah).

Rabī' Al-Thani (رَبِيعُ الثَّانِي) ▼

Rabī' Al-Thani is also known as Rabi' al-Akhir (the last spring). The same may be said concerning Rabee‘ al-Akhir as was said concerning Rabee‘ al-Awwal.

Jumādá Al-Ūlá (جُمَادَى الأُولَى) ▼

Jumādá Al-Ūlá can be deinfed as "the first of parched land,". Jumādá is so called because water froze during that month (jumood means freezing).

Jumada Al-Thaniah (جُمَادَى الثّانِيَة) ▼

Jumada al-Thaniah, also known as Jumada al-Ākhirah, means "the last of the parched land." Like Jumada al-Ula, it reflects the climatic conditions during this time, emphasizing the dry or frozen nature of the environment.

Rajab (رَجَب) 🔴▼

Rajab comes from the root meaning "to respect" or "venerate." It is another sacred month in which fighting was forbidden. Pre-Islamic Arabs would remove the heads of their spears to avoid combat. The name also implies "removal," reflecting this practice of ceasing hostilities.

Sha'bān (شعبان) ▼

The word Sha'ban means "scattered" or "dispersed," referring to the time when Arab tribes would disperse to raid others.

Ramadan (رَمَضَان) 🌙 ▼

Ramadan derives from the root word for "intense heat" or "burning," symbolizing the spiritual purification that occurs through fasting. Another meaning refers to the intense thirst of camels, drawing parallels to the self-restraint of Muslims during the month. Ramadan is the month of fasting and includes Laylat al-Qadr, the Night of Decree, one of the holiest nights in Islam.

Shawwāl (شَوَّال) ▼

Shawwāl means "raised," and is often associated with camels lifting their tails during mating season. This month follows Ramadan and is marked by the festival of Eid al-Fitr, as well as six recommended fasts.

Dhūl-Qa'dah (ذُو القَعدَة) 🔴▼

Dhūl-Qa'dah The name Dhul-Qi'dah is derived from the word qu‘ood (to sit or rest). It is a sacred month in which fighting and traveling were traditionally halted. This time was used for rest and peace before the onset of Dhul-Hijjah, the month of pilgrimage.

Dhul-Hijjah (ذُو الحِجَّة) 🔴 🕋▼

Dhul-Hijjah, meaning "the month of pilgrimage," is the final month of the Islamic calendar and the month in which the Hajj pilgrimage is performed. Key days in this month include the Day of Arafah (9th) and Eid al-Adha (10th). It is also a sacred month, where battles are prohibited, making it one of the holiest periods in the year.
